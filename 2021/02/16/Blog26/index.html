fourth day with unwind

In today’s reading, “triplicate” has gone into exile. I believe love has silently come to Risa and Connor and Lev won’t block their plan currently. Risa gets some clothes and food from suburbia brilliantly meanwhile a 19-year-old woman has just abandoned her newborn in the same place. I suppose a collision of destiny will soon happen on them coincidently. Also, something curious come out: the newspaper only reports the pileup incident on interstate rather than the escape of three AWOLS. In the last paragraph of reading, maybe another turning point emerges, which may lead to the arrest of “triplicate”. Here are some sentences which I think are full of philosophy.

  1. “So, what’s it like, knowing all your life you are going to be sacrificed?”

​ “It is better than going through life without knowing you purpose.”

​ Connor: Maybe he’s finally starting to see the sense of staying alive.

It’s true that we should lead a life with explicit purpose. But I do not think not owning a purpose towards life means your life meaningless. There’s always a purpose that we have toward life, however unrealized by us, which is being alive. What Lev says means if we don’t have a purpose, than we ought to give up our essential purpose. Apparently, this is a logic fallacy of contradiction. Moreover, living-beings are born with the desire of discovering beauty, which could also be everyone’s purpose that makes our life meaningful. Generally, nothing is better than sacrificing our own life unless being active or voluntary.

  1. Woman: How wonderful it is that she can dismiss her responsibility so easily.

Currently, the woman think it is an excellent way to get rid of the newborn without being guilty. However, I convince there will be retribution for her, from the society. Even though the woman escape from her personal suffer in the future, this kind of demeanor would definitely induce tensions in the society.

  1. “You can’t change laws without first changing human nature.”

    “You can’t change human nature without first changing the law.”

Technically Laws are made by human, so only when human nature change can the laws themselves be changed. However, meanwhile all the laws, including some local traditions would generally influence someone’s thoughts and identity. Thus the combination of the two sentences is a paradox. They can both be true and rational, but I support the first one to a great extent. The change of human nature partially depends on the laws indeed, but the intelligent people are always thinking, and unwinds are revolting. When the tensions in society are outraged greatly, the law can also be changed. And for the first one, it is not completely correct of course. Such laws can be changed, but we don’t know it is the law makers’ nature that is changing or the citizens’.
