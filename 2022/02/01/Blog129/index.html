Mary Oliver's Crossing the Swamp

Sometimes the imageries in poetry not only create a poetic vibe, but also symbolize a reflection in reality. Swamp, from time to time “misanthropic” and ticklish and stark, works as a metaphor of life obstacles that the speaker needs to overcome in a poem. Therefore, the attitude towards this stinking mud implicitly reflects the speaker’s grit when facing problems. In Mary Oliver’s artful poem Crossing the Swamp, she initially develops an intense conflict and ultimately a perfect harmony between the speaker and the swamp by using techniques like figurative language and diction.

The use of figurative language like alliteration, end rhyme, and similar syntax effectively conveys a gradually-rising optimism towards the swamp. In the beginning, the poet depicts choking “belching” “bogs” that “the dark burred faintly” and the scary “branching vines”, with the alliteration of “b” sound repeated for four times which aggravates the intensity of a disgusting and horrifying panorama of the swamp. The speaker’s later “struggle” and “closure” then naturally emerge that explicitly reveals the speaker’s misery for involving in this catastrophe hell, which can be exemplified as considering the mud as “pathless”, “seamless”, and “peerless”. This repetition of end rhymes emphasizes the tremendous devastation that is deluge of power, implying the speaker’s endless helplessness and hopelessness. Through the lines with negative mood, Oliver also uses a similar syntax with the same beginning of “here is” to state the existence of “swamp” and “struggle”, consistently demonstrating the swamp’s overwhelming oppression to the speaker. Overall, readers can apparently witness a conspicuous conflict between the speaker and the devastating mud. Furtherly speaking, human beings are struggling against those obstacles with great anguish.

Nevertheless, the intense relationship is gradually replaced by a sincerely awe to mother nature as an adroit selection of vivid dictions reveal. From the speaker’s point of view, the mires are not “slick” and “slack earthsoup” anymore, but “fat grassy” “succulent marrows” and “a breathing place”. The branches are not eroding in “the dark burred faintly belching bogs” anymore, but sprouting in “the whims of swamp water”. And the speaker even feels “painted” and “glittered”, instead of “dark” and “slack”. The conspicuous comparison points out speaker’s dramatic shift of attitude against the swamp, and more importantly, paints a sudden realization of the grandeur of the great land. The speaker originally believes that it is a dungeon taking his life, but now more firmly believes that it is a paradise of life. There is no fear of being swallowed to death anymore, but a deep appreciation towards the “nugget of dense sap”, so lively, such a gift of God, that offers no willingness in escaping, but an acceptance of human-nature convergence. This is stage when human beings gratefully respect learn from, and get used to the company of the obstacles rather than losing themselves when confronting distraction and pain that they would bring. This is rational psychological portray that helps overcome any difficulty.

Human beings and the nature, seeming to stand on two distinct sides superficially, go against each other in our common belief. However, thanks to Oliver’s Crossing the swamp, we get to realize that the veneration to the land stimulates the convergence of twain, which also suggests that our life is seamlessly intertwined with obstacles. There is not way to avoid problems. What we need to do is to fully respect and admire their existence.
